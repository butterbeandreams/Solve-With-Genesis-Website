Proof, Not Promises

Track record.

Seven engagements, one high-stakes environment: $20M budget, 50+ sites, a permanent team that never exceeded eight. The kind of range most consultants only accumulate across many clients — vendor strategy, governance design, executive communication, organizational change, cost discipline, security culture, and quantitative risk management — each with a different problem and a different fight to win.

Vendor Strategy

Said no to a multi-million-dollar vendor platform

Situation
Site vendors proposed prepackaged industrial security platforms sized for far larger plants — heavy infrastructure, heavy headcount, and locked to one manufacturer's ecosystem. Leadership was being sold a one-stop solution before anyone had asked what the site actually needed.
Approach
Designed a bespoke, automatable alternative that worked across every vendor's equipment — GE, Siemens, and others — and ran with minimal staff. Brought the VP of generation's own reports into the recommendation, so the case didn't rest on cyber's word alone.
Result
Delivered the required outcomes for under 10% of the proposed cost, across 50+ sites, with far less infrastructure and headcount to maintain.
The underlying question: how do you know when an expensive enterprise solution is the wrong answer — regardless of industry?
Governance Design

Turned a confusing audit into a standard anyone could run

Situation
The organization faced overlapping regulatory and customer requirements — including NERC CIP for generation assets — each managed as its own compliance silo. That meant duplicated effort, inconsistent controls, and a program that got harder to scale every time a new rule appeared.
Approach
After mapping business units, critical processes, and risk tolerances, selected NIST CSF as the single organizing framework, with different implementation levels so the same control language applied at the right intensity across generation, trading, and corporate environments. Mapped NERC CIP requirements directly into the NIST control set, so one body of evidence satisfied both current obligations and broader organizational needs.
Result
One framework replaced several parallel compliance programs. Audits became repeatable and automatable, and future regulatory or customer requirements could be absorbed by adjusting implementation levels — not by standing up a new program from scratch.
The underlying question: how do you design governance that adapts as regulations change, instead of rebuilding it every time they do?
Executive Communication

Turned two years of avoidance into a monthly habit

Situation
A VP of generation had no regular visibility into risk across his sites, and had avoided a standing risk meeting for two years despite repeated requests.
Approach
Built risk reporting that translated technical exposure into dollar-loss and probability — the language his own board already used — instead of vulnerability counts and severity scores.
Result
Monthly risk reviews became standard practice within that VP's organization, owned by the business rather than imposed by cyber.
The underlying question: how do you get a busy executive to prioritize a risk conversation they've been actively avoiding?
Organizational Change

Won internal audit against leadership that had already rejected it

Situation
Executives had previously refused to fund an internal audit function and declined to formally assign risk ownership via a responsibility matrix — both seen as bureaucratic overhead in a culture that actively discouraged documentation.
Approach
Made the case in terms of what leadership already cared about: clear ownership when something went wrong, and audit findings the business could act on instead of dispute. Didn't ask for authority — built the value case until leadership assigned it.
Result
Both the audit department and a formal ownership matrix were established and adopted org-wide, changing how the company assigned accountability for risk going forward.
The underlying question: how do you change organizational behavior when the culture actively resists governance?
Cost Discipline

Built a capability map that stopped redundant purchases as a pattern, not a one-off

Situation
Departments were independently evaluating and purchasing security and IT tools with no enterprise view of what already existed — creating overlapping tools, under-utilized licenses, integration debt, and a standing risk of buying expensive point solutions that duplicated features already owned.
Approach
Built a comprehensive tool and control inventory mapping every security-related capability in use, or under consideration, against:
  • The specific risks or control objectives it addressed.
  • Ownership, cost, usage statistics, and integration points.
  • Overlaps with existing native or already-licensed platforms.
Made it a required input to change-management and technical assessment reviews, so any new proposal — including auditor-recommended specialized software — was checked against what the organization already owned before it went further.
Result
Multiple high-cost purchases were avoided or significantly scaled back. In one case, a proposed multi-million-dollar compliance reporting platform — plus two new hires to run it — was replaced by mapping the requirement into the existing NIST control framework and automating reports on native Microsoft tools already owned. The same inventory kept other departments from acquiring oversized SaaS platforms the organization couldn't have supported at scale.
The underlying question: how do you build a habit of checking what you already own before buying more?
Security Culture

Turned the workforce into a security asset instead of the biggest risk

Situation
A permanent team that never exceeded eight people couldn't rely on technical controls alone — phishing and human error stay the biggest risk in any organization, regardless of how mature the technical program gets, and traditional annual training doesn't move that needle.
Approach
Right-sized the toolset using AI and automation to extend what a small team could cover, then built a direct channel to the workforce: regular phishing simulations, short reward-based lessons instead of long mandatory modules, and an internal portal where employees could ask questions, flag concerns, and find practical tips — deliberately extended to home network and family security, not just workplace policy.
Result
Built a two-way relationship between the security team and the workforce instead of a one-way policy push — employees engaged with tips for their own homes, and that trust carried over into better workplace reporting and compliance.
The underlying question: how do you make an entire workforce a security asset without growing the security team to do it?
Risk Quantification

Turned a 1,000-item risk spreadsheet into a portfolio leadership could act on

Situation
The organization had accumulated thousands of individual IT and cybersecurity findings scattered across spreadsheets — exhaustive but unusable, with inconsistent scoring, no clear linkage to business impact, and fragmented evidence that made every compliance audit painful.
Approach
Designed a unified system on a hybrid of NIST RMF process structure and FAIR quantitative analysis:
  • Consolidated the entire inventory and eliminated duplicates through structured interviews with business unit leaders and system owners across multiple sites.
  • Mapped each risk to the actual business processes and assets it could affect.
  • Scored every risk on two axes — risk level (FAIR-style frequency and magnitude of loss) and remediation difficulty (cost, complexity, resource demand, operational impact).
  • Grouped related risks sharing a common root cause into single remediation projects instead of treating them one-by-one.
  • Fed the prioritized portfolio into executive and board-level risk reporting as part of a repeatable governance cadence.
Result
A living, prioritized portfolio replaced the unmanageable spreadsheet. Leadership could see exactly where limited resources would cut the most business risk, and the system supported both current compliance obligations — including NERC CIP — and future regulatory change without starting over each time.
The underlying question: how do you turn an overwhelming list of technical findings into a short list of decisions leadership can actually act on?
