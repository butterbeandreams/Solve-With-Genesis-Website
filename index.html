Engineering

METHOD

How I think.

I don't start with frameworks or technology. I start with the business — what it depends on, what it can tolerate losing, and who's accountable for that decision.

01

Understand the business

Meet every business unit. Learn what they produce, who depends on them, and how much risk they can actually tolerate.

02

Map to one standard

Choose a single framework that clears every current compliance bar — and the ones coming next — instead of managing each rule separately.

03

Right-size the build

Set a different implementation level per business unit. The framework sets the bar; the business sets the pace.

04

Govern it to last

Name an owner, build the audit function, and put a review board between the org and the next expensive bad idea.

PROOF, NOT PROMISES

Track record.

Built and run as CISO for a lean, globally distributed generation, trading, and site-development organization.

Turned a confusing audit into a standard anyone could run

Situation
The compliance team wanted to audit every generation site against dense, constantly-shifting NERC CIP language, site by site.
Approach
Spent several years building the case for NIST CSF as the org's single standard — one framework that satisfied CIP today and scaled to new privacy and reporting rules tomorrow.
Result
Audits became repeatable and automatable, on a standard that covered the whole org — not just the compliance team.

Said no to a multi-million-dollar vendor platform

Situation
Site vendors proposed prepackaged industrial security platforms sized for far larger plants — heavy infrastructure, heavy headcount, and locked to one manufacturer's ecosystem.
Approach
Designed a bespoke, automatable alternative that worked across every vendor's equipment and ran with minimal staff.
Result
Delivered the required outcomes for under 10% of the proposed cost, across 50+ sites.

Turned two years of avoidance into a monthly habit

Situation
A VP of generation had no regular visibility into risk across his sites, and had avoided the conversation for two years.
Approach
Built risk reporting that translated technical exposure into dollar-loss and probability — the language his own board already used.
Result
Monthly risk reviews became standard practice, owned by the business rather than imposed by cyber.
