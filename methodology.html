Method

I don't start with frameworks or technology.

I start with the business — what it depends on, what it can tolerate losing, and who's accountable for that decision. Every engagement runs the same four steps, in the same order, whether the client is a 50-site utility or a 40-person startup.

01

Understand the business

Meet every business unit. Learn what they produce, who depends on them, and how much risk they can actually tolerate.

This means sitting with generation, trading, development, finance, and communications — not just IT — and mapping who consumes what, and what breaks downstream if a given unit goes dark. Risk tolerance isn't uniform across a company, and treating it as if it were is the fastest way to over-spend in some places and leave real gaps in others.
02

Map to one standard

Choose a single framework that clears every current compliance bar — and the ones coming next — instead of managing each rule separately.

Regulators rarely hand you a framework that's easy to audit. NIST CSF, applied deliberately, can satisfy a dense compliance regime like NERC CIP today while giving you a foundation that flexes for the next round of privacy or reporting rules — without re-litigating your entire program every time a regulation changes.
03

Right-size the build

Set a different implementation level per business unit. The framework sets the bar; the business sets the pace.

A generation control center and a corporate communications team don't carry the same risk, so they shouldn't carry the same security burden. Right-sizing means every dollar spent is tied to a specific, named risk — not a vendor's idea of best practice for a company three times your size.
04

Govern it to last

Name an owner, build the audit function, and put a review board between the org and the next expensive bad idea.

Programs decay without ownership. That means a solutions review board that says yes or no to new tools based on security, risk, supportability, and change management together — not whichever department shouted loudest — and an audit function independent enough to report compliance honestly, including when the answer is uncomfortable.
In Plain Terms

How I explain it to a room that isn't technical.

Analogies that have actually worked in front of boards, generation leadership, and site engineers.

Security spending is like
insurance, not renovation. You're not trying to make the building nicer — you're trying to make sure a specific, named risk doesn't sink the business. Spend past that point and you're just paying for peace of mind you didn't need to buy.
Risk reporting is like
a weather forecast, not a verdict. A dollar amount and a probability — not "the sky is falling" and not "everything's fine." That's the only version a business leader can actually act on.
Governance is like
a building code, not a design review board. It doesn't tell you what to build — it tells you what has to be true before you're allowed to build it. Everyone keeps their own decisions; governance just sets the floor.
Scope of Work

Where I can help.

Six capability areas, each grounded in the work behind the case studies — not a generic services menu.

01

Security program design & governance. Choosing a framework, setting maturity levels by business unit, and standing up the audit and review functions that keep a program alive past year one.

02

Vendor & technology evaluation. Assessing whether a proposed platform or managed service actually fits the business — including the cost and supportability tradeoffs vendors don't lead with.

03

Executive & board risk communication. Translating technical exposure into dollar-loss, probability, and a plain-language recommendation a board can actually act on.

04

Regulatory & compliance strategy. Mapping overlapping obligations — like NERC CIP — onto one practical framework instead of managing each requirement separately.

05

Budget & resource planning. Structuring spend across internal staff, contractors, and managed service providers to match actual risk — not vendor upsell.

06

Governance adoption & organizational change. Building support for ownership matrices, review boards, and internal audit in cultures that don't already have them.

07

Lean operations through automation. Right-sizing the toolset itself — using AI and automation to extend what a small permanent team can cover, instead of solving coverage gaps by adding headcount.

08

Security awareness & culture programs. Turning end users into a security asset — phishing simulation, short reward-based training, and self-service resources that build trust instead of just enforcing policy.

09

Risk quantification & portfolio management. Turning a sprawling, inconsistently scored risk inventory into a prioritized, living portfolio — using NIST RMF and FAIR-style quantitative scoring so trade-offs hold up under executive scrutiny.
