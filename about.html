Cybersecurity is a supporting function — same as it's always been. My job is making sure it acts like one.

I was asked to build a security program with almost no guidance beyond a minimum bar: keep the generation control center compliant with NERC CIP. Everything past that, I had to figure out myself — starting with the business, not the technology.

That meant sitting down with every business unit — generation, trading, site development, legal, finance, HR, communications — to understand what each one actually did, who depended on them, what they produced or consumed, and how much risk they could tolerate. From that, I mapped the whole company and picked a single framework, NIST CSF, that could meet every current compliance requirement and scale to whatever came next: new CIP rules, new privacy law, new reporting demands.

I built an audit department to report on both NERC and NIST compliance, and turned a spreadsheet of more than a thousand disconnected risk findings into a living, prioritized portfolio using NIST RMF and FAIR-style quantitative scoring — so spending decisions were driven by actual loss exposure, not gut feel. I put in place governance — including a solutions review board — in a culture that had never had one, and where our own parent company regularly pushed products and vendors onto us that didn't fit our business model. We rejected most of them, and explained clearly enough why that leadership backed the decision.

The recurring theme, across almost every fight I had as CISO — with auditors, with vendors, with other departments — was the same one: somebody wanted to spend money or enforce a rule without first understanding what the business actually needed. My job was translation. Executives think in outcomes; engineers think in solutions. I move between the two, using plain analogies instead of jargon, so decisions get made by the people accountable for them.

I've since taken that same approach independent, working with organizations who need CISO-level judgment on a fractional basis — without the overhead, and without a bigger security program than their actual risk calls for.

Too many cyber people think they're more than a support for the business. My job was always to make sure the business came first.
